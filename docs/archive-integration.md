# Archive Integration Strategy

Project RUN should treat existing digital archives as partners and indexes, not as competitors.

## Purpose

The physical collection provides provenance, condition, material evidence, and personal context. Public digital collections can provide full-text search, page-level access, downloadable files, and broader coverage. Linking the two creates a more useful record than either can provide alone.

## Internet Archive

Internet Archive is a likely primary source for magazines, manuals, catalogs, software, and recordings. A Project RUN record may link to one or more Archive.org items when they represent the same edition or a closely related source.

Each link should record:

- the external identifier and canonical URL;
- what the external item contains;
- whether it appears to match the physical holding;
- scan or file quality notes;
- access and rights information when known;
- the date the link was checked.

## Do not assume identity

A matching title and date do not guarantee that a scan is the same edition, printing, regional variant, or bundled version. Records should distinguish:

- exact match;
- probable match;
- related edition;
- partial or supplementary material;
- unknown relationship.

## Local preservation

External links are discovery aids, not a substitute for responsible preservation. Project RUN may retain local derivatives, metadata, checksums, or permitted copies when this improves durability, research, or accessibility. Any copying must respect applicable rights and source terms.

## Future indexing

Potential future work includes:

- importing tables of contents from trustworthy sources;
- indexing article titles, authors, products, companies, and advertisements;
- connecting software listings to runnable disk images or transcriptions;
- generating search pages that combine local holdings with external digital access;
- recording gaps where no usable digital surrogate exists.

## Source priority

Prefer, in order:

1. direct observation of the physical item;
2. publisher or institutional records;
3. reputable public archives and library catalogs;
4. well-documented community databases;
5. marketplace listings or unsourced pages, used only as leads.

Every imported assertion should retain enough source information to be checked later.
