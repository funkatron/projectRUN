# Work Areas

Project RUN is organized around three overlapping kinds of work. These are not departments or strict boundaries; a single object may move through all three.

## 1. The Collection

The Collection is the physical and digital inventory.

It records hardware, software, peripherals, books, magazines, manuals, packaging, removable media, recordings, and related ephemera. Each record should capture identity, condition, provenance, completeness, location, value when useful, and the stories attached to the item.

The goal is not merely to know what is owned. It is to preserve enough evidence that someone can understand what the item is, where it came from, how it was used, and what remains to be done with it.

Typical work:

- inventorying and photographing items;
- recording condition, provenance, and storage location;
- identifying duplicates, missing parts, and preservation risks;
- tracking acquisition, disposal, lending, and valuation;
- linking related objects such as a computer, its manuals, and its software.

## 2. The Archive

The Archive is the documentary and research layer around the collection.

It connects physical holdings to scans, advertisements, reviews, manuals, software images, recordings, oral histories, catalogs, contemporary prices, and other external sources. It also records where digital versions already exist, especially on Internet Archive and other public repositories.

The Archive should make the collection searchable beyond the information printed on an object. A magazine issue, for example, may eventually be searchable by article, author, product, company, advertisement, program listing, and subject.

Typical work:

- locating and evaluating digital surrogates;
- indexing tables of contents and article metadata;
- documenting source quality and rights status;
- preserving research notes and citations;
- connecting items across publications, software, companies, and people.

## 3. The Workbench

The Workbench is where preserved material becomes active.

It includes restoration, testing, imaging disks, running software, reproducing workflows, comparing historical claims, recording demonstrations, building tools, and creating new work from old systems. The Workbench also produces interpretation: essays, exhibits, videos, music, visual art, and software.

Typical work:

- safely testing and repairing hardware;
- imaging and verifying removable media;
- reproducing period-appropriate setups;
- documenting procedures and results;
- building catalog, search, and publishing tools;
- developing public stories and creative projects.

## How they connect

A magazine can be cataloged in **The Collection**, linked to a digital scan and indexed in **The Archive**, then used to reproduce a type-in program or investigate an advertisement in **The Workbench**.

The project is strongest when these areas reinforce one another: objects provide evidence, archives provide context, and hands-on work produces understanding.
